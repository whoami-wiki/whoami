---
title: Editorial Standards
description: "Editorial principles, writing style, and conventions for wiki content"
---

These standards guide how pages in a ProjectWiki encyclopedia should be written. Whether you're writing by hand or reviewing output from an AI agent, the goal is the same: a consistent, readable encyclopedia that treats construction project documentation with the same rigor as any other technical subject.

## One canonical home

Every piece of content lives in exactly one place. Other pages link to it; they don't duplicate it. If two process areas share the same influent pipe, the pipe details live on one page. Each area page links to it with a sentence or two of context. It doesn't get described in full on both.

## Prefer splitting to growing

When in doubt, make a new page. An equipment item that takes more than two paragraphs to describe properly deserves its own page. The area page becomes a hub of links, not a monolith of content.

## Documentary voice on area pages

Third person, present tense for design parameters, past tense for construction events. The area page reads like a technical encyclopedia article. "The primary clarifiers receive raw wastewater from the headworks," not "This area gets flow from upstream." No direct address to the reader. No editorial commentary on whether a design decision was good or bad.

## Drawing pages follow observations before interpretation

Drawing pages have a strict protocol: record physical observations before providing engineering interpretation. This prevents confirmation bias and ensures nothing is missed. Observations describe what is physically on the drawing — dimensions, callouts, notes, symbols. Interpretation comes only after observations are complete.

## Don't interpret for the reader

An encyclopedia states facts. It does not tell the reader how to feel about them, inflate their significance, or narrate their broader meaning. This is the single most important quality standard for the encyclopedia, and the one that requires the most vigilance during editing. Several specific habits undermine it:

### Don't editorialize

Words like "staggering," "extraordinary," "critical," "spectacular," and "surgical precision" are editorial judgments dressed up as description. Replace them with specifics that let the reader draw their own conclusions.

> **Before:** The foundation design is extraordinarily complex with a staggering number of reinforcement details.
>
> **After:** The foundation requires 47 reinforcement details across 12 drawing sheets, with rebar spacing varying from 6" to 12" O.C.

The fix is almost always the same: replace the adjective with the specific fact that prompted it. If there's no specific fact behind the adjective, cut it.

### Don't inflate significance

Resist the urge to explain why something matters by connecting it to a broader theme. Phrases like "marking a pivotal phase," "reflecting a broader design philosophy," "underscoring the importance of," or "setting the stage for" add no information. If something is significant, the facts will demonstrate it without a caption.

> **Before:** The dewatering system marks a pivotal element of the construction sequencing, reflecting the broader importance of groundwater management.
>
> **After:** Dewatering must be operational before excavation below elevation 98.5 ft. Groundwater was measured at elevation 100.2 ft in boring B-7.

### Don't use promotional language

Writing about construction projects can drift into admiration, especially around innovative designs or complex systems. Keep the register neutral. Avoid language that reads like a project brochure. Words like "state-of-the-art," "innovative," "world-class," "showcasing," and "cutting-edge" belong in marketing materials, not encyclopedias.

### Don't attribute vaguely

When stating a claim, be specific about where it comes from. Avoid vague attributions like "industry standards require," "best practices suggest," or "it is generally accepted that" when you can cite a specific specification paragraph or drawing note. If no specific source exists for a claim, that's a sign the claim may not belong in the encyclopedia.

## Prose quality

Good encyclopedic prose is plain, varied, and direct. The following guidelines address common problems.

### Say "is" when you mean "is"

Prefer simple copulatives over elaborate substitutions. "The basin is 120 feet in diameter" is better than "The basin stands as a 120-foot diameter structure." Reserve "serves as" and "stands as" for cases where something literally serves a function distinct from what it is.

### Keep sentences short

If a sentence exceeds roughly 40 words, it probably needs to be split. Long sentences with multiple subordinate clauses make the reader do too much work. A good opening paragraph looks like this:

> Area 03 encompasses the primary clarifier complex consisting of four 120-foot diameter circular clarifiers. The clarifiers receive raw wastewater from the headworks via a 48-inch reinforced concrete influent pipe. Settled sludge is pumped to the thickening facility via dedicated sludge lines.

### Vary rhythm and structure

Monotonous prose reads as lifeless: every sentence the same length, every paragraph the same shape. Mix short declarative sentences with longer ones. Let some paragraphs be two sentences and others be five. The pacing should follow the material, not a template.

### Use punctuation precisely

Em dashes, colons, semicolons, and parentheses each have a job. Problems arise when one punctuation mark (especially the em dash) gets used as a Swiss Army knife for every purpose. When one mark does every job, it stops doing any job well.

### Don't cycle through synonyms

When referring to the same thing multiple times, it's fine to repeat the word. Cycling through synonyms to avoid repetition ("the clarifier," "the basin," "the sedimentation tank," "the settling structure") creates the impression of variety while actually making the text harder to follow. If you said "clarifier" the first time, say "clarifier" again.

### Avoid formulaic transitions

Overusing connectives like "moreover," "furthermore," "notably," "additionally" (especially at the start of a sentence) creates a stiff, essay-like tone. Good prose doesn't need constant signposting. Let the paragraph structure do the connecting work.

### Don't end sections with summaries

If a section is well-written, it doesn't need a concluding sentence that restates its core idea. Phrases like "In summary," "Overall," and "In conclusion" signal that the section didn't make its point clearly enough the first time.

## Words to watch

Certain words and phrases appear so frequently in low-quality encyclopedic prose that they've become red flags during editing. This is not a banned word list. Context matters. But when these appear, it's worth pausing to ask whether the sentence is actually saying something, or just performing the act of saying something.

**Significance words:** pivotal, crucial, vital, key (as adjective), fundamental, instrumental, transformative, groundbreaking, indelible, enduring, profound, testament

**Promotional words:** state-of-the-art, innovative, world-class, vibrant, renowned, nestled, boasts, showcases, exemplifies, stunning, remarkable, extraordinary, cutting-edge

**Empty intensifiers:** genuine/genuinely, truly, deeply, incredibly, remarkably, undeniable, unmistakable

**Vague framing:** it's important to note, it is worth noting, no discussion would be complete without, reflecting a broader trend

**Inflated verbs:** stands as, serves as (when "is" will do), marks/represents (a turning point), underscores, highlights (as verb), fosters, encompasses

**Superficial connectors:** moreover, furthermore, notably, additionally (sentence-initial), on the other hand, in terms of

When one of these appears, the fix is usually to delete the word or phrase and see if the sentence still works. It almost always does.

## Voice and register

Area and equipment pages use encyclopedic voice: third person, neutral. No rhetorical questions. No direct address. State what the design requires, in what order, with what evidence.

Drawing pages follow the observations-before-interpretation protocol. Spec pages preserve verbatim contract language using `{{Verbatim}}` blocks. Construction document pages (RFIs, submittals) maintain factual records of questions, responses, and traceability.

## Quoting conventions

Direct quotes from contract documents are valuable primary source material. Use `{{Verbatim}}` blocks when:

- The exact contract language matters (scope definitions, performance criteria, warranty terms)
- The phrasing has contractual significance and can't be paraphrased without losing meaning
- The language may be relevant to change order or claim arguments

Don't use verbatim blocks for:

- Routine procedural statements that can be paraphrased
- Padding: three verbatim blocks in a row saying similar things
- General requirements that are standard across all projects

Use `{{Verbatim}}` blocks for extended passages that need to stand alone. For shorter references, integrate the specification language into prose with a citation to the source paragraph.
